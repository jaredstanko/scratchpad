A family bucket list card deck — 18 cards across 3 pages

A Family Bucket List Deck
What's Alive
In You
Not what you should want.
What you actually want.
How To Use This Deck
  1. Phase 1 (Solo): Each person takes the 12 prompt cards privately. Write down whatever comes to mind. No filtering. No one sees this yet.
  2. Phase 2 (Reveal): Come together. Each person shares 5–7 items. The only allowed response is "Tell me more about…"
  3. Phase 3 (Venn): Sort everything into Solo, Shared, and Family lists. All three matter equally.

There are no wrong answers. Weird is good. Small is good. "Unrealistic" is good.
Phase 1
Solo Expansion
Take the 12 prompt cards somewhere alone. A bedroom, a coffee shop, a walk.

Read each card. Write whatever comes. Don't judge it. Don't ask "is this realistic?" Just capture what surfaces.

Time: 30–60 minutes, or spread across a few days.

The rule: Nobody sees your answers until Phase 2. This is yours first.
Phase 2
The Family Reveal
Sit together. No phones. Each person shares 5–7 things from their lists.

The only rule: When someone shares, you may only respond with:
• "Tell me more about that."
• A genuine question.

Forbidden: "That's cool" (dismissive), "Why?" (interrogating), "We should do that together!" (hijacking).

Just listen. Let each person's desires exist on their own terms.
Phase 3
The Venn
After everyone has shared, three lists emerge naturally:

Solo — Things that are yours alone. These matter. A bucket list isn't only shared experiences.

Overlap — Things two or more people share. These are gold—natural allies for making it happen.

Family — Things you want to experience together specifically.

Your family bucket list is the union of all three.
Ground Rules
  1. No size filter. "Learn to whistle with two fingers" sits next to "live abroad for a year." Both count.
  2. No judgment. Not of yourself, not of each other.
  3. Solo items are sacred. Nobody gets to decide someone else's desires aren't "family enough."
  4. Revisit yearly. What's alive in you changes. That's the point.
  5. Start one within a week. Pick the smallest, most doable item from anyone's list and do it. Momentum matters more than planning.
The Envy Signal
01
When you see someone doing something and feel a pang of jealousy or longing—what are they doing?
Envy is the most honest signal you have. It bypasses your rational filter completely. Don't judge what comes up. Write it down.
The Deathbed Regret
02
You're 85 years old, looking back. What do you regret never trying? Not never doing—never trying.
The distinction matters. We rarely regret failed attempts. We regret the things we never started.
The Secret Self
03
If nobody you know would ever find out, what would you spend an entire year doing?
This removes social pressure, expectations, and identity. What's left is closer to what you actually want.
The Mastery Pull
04
If you woke up tomorrow having completely mastered one skill, and it changed how you see yourself—what is it?
Not "what would be useful." What skill would shift your identity? That pull toward mastery is telling you something.
The Witness Desire
05
What do you want to see happen in the world, in nature, in science, in history—even if you play no role in making it happen?
Not everything on a bucket list requires you to act. Some of the deepest desires are about witnessing.
The Creation Itch
06
What do you want to make that doesn't exist yet? A thing, a piece of art, a business, a place, a community—anything.
Humans are creators. This question finds the thing you want to bring into the world, not just experience in it.
The Fear Flip
07
What scares you that, if you did it, would make you a fundamentally different person on the other side?
Fear and desire are often the same signal. The things that scare us most are frequently the things that would grow us most.
The Recurring Dream
08
What keeps showing up? A place you think about, a type of day you keep imagining, a version of your life that flickers in your mind?
Recurring thoughts are data. If your mind keeps returning somewhere, it's trying to tell you something. What is it?
The Body Question
09
What do you want your body to be able to do? Not look like. Do.
Run a trail. Hold a handstand. Swim across a lake. Dance without thinking. Your body is a vehicle—where does it want to go?
The Understanding Hunger
10
What do you want to deeply understand? A subject, a culture, a person, a system, a language, how something really works?
Not "learn about." Understand. The kind of knowing where you could explain it to anyone and feel it in your bones.
The Contribution Mark
11
If you could solve one problem—for your street, your school, your city, or the entire world—what would it be?
Scale doesn't matter. "Fix the broken bench at the park" and "end loneliness" are both valid. What problem pulls at you?
The Texture Question
12
Describe a moment you want to experience. Not an event. A moment. What does the air feel like? What do you hear? Where are you?
This bypasses the "bucket list item" framing entirely. It gets at the felt experience you're actually chasing.
